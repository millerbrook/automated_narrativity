## Robert Long — "Language models are different from humans, and that's okay" (*Experience Machines*, Apr 1, 2026)

Bib key: `long2026experience`. Full text not reproduced here (copyright) — summary and a couple of short excerpts below; re-read the original on Substack when drafting slides.

**Argument, in brief:** Long pushes back on two symmetric bad-faith framings of LLM cognition — the "Sneerer," who treats LLM weirdness/error as proof they can't be conscious, and the "Defender," who waves away that weirdness by pointing out humans are weird and error-prone too. Both moves shut down the more interesting question of *how exactly* LLMs and humans differ (evolutionary history, embodiment, discrete vs. continuous existence, copyability, language-first vs. experience-first development) and what those specific differences imply. He draws an analogy to animal cognition research, which only progressed once researchers stopped measuring animals against a human standard and took their cognition on its own terms.

- Possible use: pairs naturally with Section "Chiang, Tufekci, and the Phenomenological Critique" as a counterpoint/complication — Long would push back on treating "plausibility machine" framing as case-closed, while also not endorsing naive equivalence claims.
- Possible use: relevant to Section "How Might a Narrative-Processing AI Differ?" — his list of concrete LLM/human differences is a good structural echo of that section's questions.

Short excerpts (verify exact wording against the source before using in slides):

> "Recognizing (while not exaggerating) our differences is essential if we want to understand the new kinds of mind that we will be sharing the world with."

> "Jaggedness is evidence that LLM intelligence is different from human intelligence in some deep ways."

## Bouizegarene, Ramstead, Constant, Friston, and Kirmayer — "Narrative as active inference: an integrative account of cognitive and social functions in adaptation" (*Frontiers in Psychology*, 2024)

Bib key: `bouizegarene2024narrative`. Was not yet in the bib — added.

> "Specifically, we propose that narratives of specific past events function as a pool of information aimed at guiding the generation of effective predictions. Well-predicted events (i.e., routines) quickly become forgotten because they do not contribute relevant information for predictions. When prediction-errors arise, they lead to the encoding of new memories, because they carry highly relevant information to improve the accuracy of predictions in the future. The formation of new memories, which necessarily involves narratives, can be understood as a process of model updating. Active inference at the level of event narratives involves a conservative process of replicating elements of past events into future events that are closely similar to those past events (e.g., habits) which maintains coherence and continuity between past and future, and a creative or constructive process that involves the flexible recombination of past events into future events. Narratives are particularly useful for active inference because they can contribute to both the conservative processes that maintain old memories and use them as models for new events, as well as generating new models through their recombination or projection to produce new stories."

- Possible use: Section "Narrative in Cognition" — direct support for the "as Active Inference" bullet
- Note: in-text citation markers from the original were stripped as empty parentheses when pasted; re-check the source for the specific supporting citations before using in a paper/handout

> "We propose that the involvement of identity narratives in individual active inference involves two processes. First, identity narratives lead to the creation and conservation of global coherence among representations in a process that can be seen as a form of active inference about the person in their local world. Second, identity narratives allow the integration of new possibilities to eventually restore a renewed sense of self-continuity and familiarity, to restore a coherent set of expectations and integrate environmental or situational changes."

- Possible use: Section "Narrative in Cognition" (identity/self-continuity angle) and Section "How Might a Narrative-Processing AI Differ?" (self-function reflection, closer semblance of self)

> "In a nutshell, we contend that certain narratives `become' master narratives because they serve an important function for the group. Specifically, master narratives serve to synchronize the predictions that group members generate for their own and others' future states, behaviors, and relationships. The fact that most group members tend to adopt the same narrative models of each other's behavior and life course (or identity) makes it easier to predict the behavior and life course of oneself and each other. When members of a culture share the same master narratives, they share a set of predictions implicitly included by this narrative; how they collectively think that events and life periods are supposed to unfold. Our model suggests that master narratives may be understood as being put in place and maintained because of their role in the synchronization of predictions in the group."

- Possible use: could extend "Narrative in Cognition" toward a social/collective dimension (master narratives as prediction-synchronization across a group) — not currently in the outline, but worth flagging as a possible addition
